Does My Business Actually
Need a Website in 2026?
The Honest Answer

March 18, 2026 By Vornela Systems

It's a fair question. You've been getting work through word of mouth for years. Your Facebook page has a few hundred followers. A mate told you his Google Business Profile gets him calls every week. Business is ticking along. So why pay for a website?

We build websites for a living, so you'd expect us to tell you that every business needs one immediately. We're not going to do that. The honest answer is more nuanced — and it depends on where your business is right now and where you want it to go.

Here's what we actually think, including the situations where a website genuinely isn't your most urgent priority.

What's Changed About How Customers Find Businesses

In 2016, word of mouth meant someone told a friend your name and they called you. In 2026, word of mouth means someone tells a friend your name and they Google you before they call.

This shift is why the conversation about websites has changed. It's not about whether customers find you through search. Many of them still come via referrals, local Facebook groups, spotted vans, and community recommendations. The change is what happens after that first touch point.

The critical stat: 87% of consumers research a business online before making contact — even when they were referred by someone they trust. They're not questioning the referral. They're looking for confirmation that you're credible, that your work looks right, and that they feel comfortable calling you.

What they find when they Google you either confirms the referral or creates doubt. A Google Business Profile with 4 reviews and no photos doesn't confirm much. A professional website with a gallery of real jobs, genuine customer testimonials, and a clear explanation of what you do and where you work — that turns a warm referral into a definite call.

The Case for Not Having a Website Yet

There are real situations where building a website isn't your immediate priority:

You're already turning work away

If you're booked out two months ahead and spending more time managing enquiries than doing jobs, adding a website will primarily generate more of the same problem. Your time is better spent on operations, pricing, or building a team. A website makes sense once you have capacity to fill.

You're in the very early stages

If you've been trading for less than a year and are still finding your footing with services, pricing, and your customer base, a website can wait. Focus on getting your first 10 customers, collecting reviews, and figuring out what you do best. Build the site once you have photos, testimonials, and a clear story to tell.

Your local market is hyper-referral based

Some trades in some areas run almost entirely on community recommendation. If you work in a tight-knit rural area where everyone knows everyone, and your referral pipeline is strong and consistent, a website is a nice-to-have rather than urgent. A fully completed Google Business Profile might be enough.

The Case for Having a Website

For most Irish tradespeople and service business owners who've been trading for more than a year and want to grow, the arguments for having a website are compelling:

You don't own your social profiles — and they can disappear

Facebook, Instagram, Google Business Profile — all of these are platforms you use, not platforms you own. Accounts get hacked, suspended, or algorithmically deprioritised. Facebook has been making it consistently harder for businesses to reach their followers organically without paying. Instagram can lock you out without notice. Google can suspend a GBP listing over a verification dispute that takes weeks to resolve.

A website you own and control is the one permanent, stable home your business has online. It exists regardless of what any platform decides to do.

Google can't send you organic search traffic without somewhere to send it

A Google Business Profile gets your business into the map pack for local searches — but Google also returns organic (non-map) results for every search, and those results are almost always websites. When someone searches "roofer Limerick", the map pack shows 3 businesses. Below it, the organic results show 10 websites. If you don't have a website, you're invisible to that entire second set of results.

Long-tail searches — "how much does a new roof cost Limerick", "do I need planning permission for an extension Kerry" — return websites almost exclusively. This is where your customers are researching before they reach out, and it's traffic that compounds over time as you build content.

It's the only channel where you can fully control your story

Your Google Business Profile shows your name, hours, and reviews. Your Facebook page shows posts. Neither lets you properly explain what makes you different, show your full portfolio, answer the 12 questions every customer has before they hire you, or build the kind of layered trust that turns a browser into a confident caller.

A website is the only place where you can control every element of how your business is perceived. What services you highlight. What jobs you feature. What your customers say about you. How straightforward it is to get in touch. All of these affect conversion rate — how many visitors actually become paying customers.

87% of customers research before they contact

We mentioned this above, but it's worth reinforcing. Even if every single one of your leads currently comes from referrals, 87% of them are Googling your name before they call. What they find shapes whether they actually call. A professional website with real photos and genuine reviews is, for most of those people, the thing that moves them from "I'll think about it" to "I'll call now."

What a Tradesperson's Website Actually Needs to Do

The mistake most tradespeople make when they do get a website built is treating it as an online business card — a place that just says you exist. A website that converts does much more than that.

The elements that drive enquiries:

  • A portfolio of real work — before-and-after photos, recent jobs with location details, work that matches what your target customers are looking for
  • Genuine reviews — names, star ratings, specific details. Vague testimonials ("great work, highly recommend") don't convince anyone. Specific ones do ("replaced our roof in Athlone in two days, came in under quote, cleaned up after themselves")
  • Frictionless contact — a form that works on mobile, a phone number that's always visible, WhatsApp if you use it
  • An FAQ section — the 8 to 10 questions every customer asks. Answering them on the page means customers arrive on the phone more qualified, more confident, and less likely to haggle on price
  • Local SEO signals — your location, service area, and relevant keywords integrated throughout — not stuffed, but natural

Built with these elements and connected to a complete Google Business Profile, a tradesperson's website becomes a compounding asset — generating enquiries every month, qualifying customers before they call, and building a digital reputation that grows over time.

The Honest Summary

If you're booked solid and turning work away: your website can wait.
If you're early-stage and still finding your feet: your website can wait.
If you've been trading for a year or more, want to grow, and your competitors have websites: you need one. Not urgently in the way that you need to fix a leak, but urgently in the way that every month without one is a month of compound growth going to someone else.

The businesses that will dominate local search in two years are the ones building their online presence now. The ones who wait until "the time is right" will be playing catch-up to a competitor who started earlier.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a website if I already have a Facebook page?

Facebook is useful for community presence and referral validation, but it doesn't rank well in Google search results for local service queries, and you don't own your content — Facebook can restrict or remove it. A website you own is the foundation. Facebook is a channel that feeds into it, not a replacement for it.
Can I use Instagram instead of a website for my trade business?

Instagram is excellent for showing your work visually, but like all social platforms, you don't own it. Accounts can be hacked, suspended, or algorithmically suppressed. It also doesn't appear in local Google search results for "plumber near me" or "electrician Cork". A website gives you a permanent, owned presence that works 24/7 without depending on any platform's algorithm.
How much does a website for a tradesperson cost in Ireland?

A professional website for a tradesperson or service business in Ireland typically costs between €1,500 and €5,000 depending on scope and features. The investment pays for itself quickly when even one or two additional jobs per month are attributed to the site. We offer a free strategy call to scope what you actually need and what it would cost.
What should a tradesperson's website include?

At minimum: a clear description of your services and service area, a gallery of recent work with before-and-after photos, genuine customer reviews with names and star ratings, a contact form and prominent phone number. A good site also includes an About section, an FAQ that handles objections before the call, and clear calls to action on every page.
Will a website actually get me more work?

A website on its own doesn't automatically generate leads — it needs to be findable via Google Business Profile or SEO, and credible enough to convert visitors into enquiries. A well-built site connected to local SEO will consistently generate inbound enquiries over time. Many of our clients see their site become their highest-volume lead source within 6 to 12 months of launch.
